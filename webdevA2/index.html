Human Anatomy

The art of understanding how the human body and limbs shape, move, and rotate.

Why Study Human Anatomy?

Being able to understand how the human body looks, moves, and rotate is essential is drawing a accurate and proportional character, especially when it comes to drawing in different perspectives and dynamic poses.

With human anatomy applied to your characters, you will be able to make your characters look more realistic, believable, and also more than just creating an artwork on a technical level, but on an emotional level with soul.

Understanding The Human Body & Simplifying Into Shapes

The torso and hips tend to be overlooked as people tend to focus on the head or hands first. However, these parts are most crucial as they dictate how the limbs and head would be positioned and rotated.

The hip can be simplified into a sort of heart-shape resemblant object, with the two circular parts of the heart locating the joint connecting to the legs, and the bottom center locating the crotch (where there is a gap between the legs).

The ribcage can be simplified into an egg-shaped object with a arch-like shape negated from the bottom center. While it might seem insignificant, the bottom edges of the side ribs indicate where the waist begins.

Proportion Guidelines & Applying Into Actual Illustrations

Box proportions are commonly used by even the best of digital illustrators to use as a guide for how big different parts should be. With Mogoon's proportions as an example, taking the width of the head as 1 unit, typically the height of the head ranges between 1.25 to 1.5, ribcage height around 1.5 to 2, pelvis height around 1 to 1.25, each arm segment length (upper and lower) being around 1.9, and leg lengths are heavily dependant on personal style. Of course, the rest of the body parts also differ from style to style and the character type you aim for, so find what fits you most! (These are all general guidelines, don't have to follow 1:1!)

Starting with box-proportion guidelines, you can apply simplified shapes of the body parts to help guide into creating more dynamic & proportionate poses and less deformative looking characters.

Dynamic Poses & Using Real-Life References

How Dynamic Poses Affect Composition & Using Reference Sources

Dynamic Poses and Using Real-Life Reference Sources

Including dynamic poses into your character sketches and illustrations is a great way to add life and flow into your work! Rather than having your characters be stiff and still, you can portray your characters in motion, or in a pose. Get as creative as you want, really, utilising what you've learnt about body proportions and anatomy to aid in drawing the human body from different angles and perspectives.

A good way to create dynamic poses for your character is simply referencing real-life images. Simply scrolling through image forums or any image sharing platforms, you can find tons of real life images of pose references! A very popular choice among hobbyists and professionals is the Pinterest app.

The trend of redrawing the viral three Japanese high schoolers posing for google maps serves as a good example for how artists utilise real-life images as reference for dynamic poses.

Applying References and Simplification

As you've seen, the human body can be simplfiied into simpler shapes, and using real-life references can greatly assist in drawing dynamic, flowy, and proportionally accurate characters, and doing so is relatively simple. Starting off with finding a reference image of a pose that you like, simply analyse the different body parts' rotations and draw the box-proportions and simplified shapes. Finally, connect it all together with a simple sketch.

Color Theory

Understanding Color Theory and Using It To Make Your Art Pop

Why Learn Color Theory?

When applied correctly, color can turn your artwork into a masterpiece. Utilising color palettes to paint emotion, contrast to draw a focus point or saturation for that vibrant pop, there is no limit to what color theory can bring out in your artwork.

Color Palettes

Color Palettes are a great guide to choosing which colors you should add to your illustration!

While there are many color palettes to choose from, the Triad is one of the most common ones used even by professionals such as Mika Pikazo.

Looking at her works, you can identify not just the triad color palette, but also the 60/30/10 rule of color distribution - having the main color be 60% of the colors with 30% supporting and 10% terminating/accent. (For example, 60% red, 30% mint green, 10% orange)

Choosing Your Colors

The first step to choosing your colors is to understand what feeling you are trying to invoke. With this illustration by Oridays, the feeling of softness is portrayed with the use of light colors, mainly white as the background, hair and collar base primary color, beige and blue for clothing and accessories as a secondary/supporting color, and pink as the accent color.

The second step is to understand color temperature and focus points. The color wheel can be split into two halves - those closer to Red (warm) and those closer to Blue. (Cool)

Warm colors may invoke stronger feelings such as passion, energy and warmth, while cooler colors may invoke more calming feelinsg like serenity, relaxation, peace.

In this example illustration of Frieren, the focus point is the character herself. To draw the viewer's attention to the character, stronger warm colors (representing the sunset cast) are used to create contrast with the softer cool colors (serene evening sky).

The last step is to understand "Black and Whites". Once again studying Mika Pikazo's illustrations, her understanding of black and whites is masterful.
Using the Values of a color (how light or dark), you can not only create contrast to draw a focus point, but to also inflict emotion through color palettes in environments.

Looking closely at the example illustration, the contrast in black and white is used to not only create a balance in the different elements to prevent invoking a "chaotic" feeling, but also to create a framing to draw attention towards the character's face and to allow the viewer to understand where to look at.
This is accomplished with the character's face having the lightest values in the illustration, framed with the low values of the hair, and the middle values of the background and other elements.

The most important part of learning art is to apply what you've learnt. That was alot, so let's doodle a little! Be open-minded to exploring different styles too!
